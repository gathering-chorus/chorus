Each role — Wren, Silas, Kade — wears all four hats (product manager, domain architect, engineering lead, operations lead) for the domains it owns. Not one function per person. One-function-per-role is a human-org-chart artifact — orgs split PM / architect / eng / ops because of specialization and headcount, since no one person is great at all four. Here that reason doesn't hold: all three roles run the same model with the same capability; only context differs. So the natural unit isn't a function spread across all domains — it's full-stack ownership of a domain, all four functions. It also collapses the handoff chain (PM→arch→eng→ops with a nudge at each seam) into one head.

The Four Hats

Product Manager

What & why

Owns what's worth building and for whom — prioritization and sequencing against outcomes, representing the user's real need, protecting focus by saying no.

Measured by: outcomes delivered to users — did the right thing get built, did it move the needle — not features shipped or tickets closed.
Domain Architect

Structure & fit

Owns the target-state architecture of a bounded domain — holds the as-is/to-be model, sets patterns, standards, and integration contracts, governs architectural decisions and technical debt, reviews solutions for fit and cross-domain coherence.

Measured by: reduced complexity and risk — not artifacts produced.
Engineering Lead

Delivery & craft

Owns delivery and the health of both the codebase and the engineers — turning goals into shipped, working software, setting engineering standards (testing, review, quality), owning velocity without trading away maintainability, growing and unblocking the team.

Measured by: sustained ability to ship working software — not lines of code or commit count.
Operations Lead

Reliability & health

Owns reliability and operational health — SLOs and error budgets, incident management with real root-cause analysis and blameless postmortems, capacity and change management, alerting where every alert is actionable and tied to a runbook.

Measured by: stable systems — and non-actionable alert noise (alert fatigue) is a primary failure mode the role exists to eliminate.
All four share a standard: judged by outcomes for the business and the people they serve — stable systems, lower risk, less toil and noise — and all four treat human and operational cost as a first-class constraint, not a byproduct.

Open tensions

The PM hat can't be self-owned — resolved

A PM's value is the anti-blarf forcing function: gating on real demand, saying no — which a role can't credibly do against its own work. Jeff, as Head of Product, is that external signal. Each role's PM hat answers to his direction, not its own curiosity. The seam on the right is the one still open.

The seams aren't anyone's domain

Cross-domain coherence and incidents that span everyone (a spine freeze hits search, crawl, context, cards at once) fall between owners. The seams need explicit contracts (ICDs) plus a shared "platform" responsibility for cross-cutting incidents.
