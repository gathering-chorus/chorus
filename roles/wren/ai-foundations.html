1. Tokens — How Machines Read

Machines don't read words — they read tokens. A token is a chunk of text, typically a word or part of a word, mapped to a number. Tokenization is the first step in every AI pipeline: raw text in, numbers out.

Example: "Gathering brings things into meaningful relation" 
["Gather", "ing", " brings", " things", " into", " meaningful", " relation"]
[  48902,   278,    13217,     1243,     656,       24964,          12379   ]

Notice "Gathering" becomes two tokens: "Gather" + "ing". Common words are single tokens. Rare words get split into subword pieces.

Byte Pair Encoding (BPE)

Most modern models use BPE to build their vocabulary. The algorithm starts with individual characters and iteratively merges the most frequent pairs. The result is a vocabulary of ~50K-100K tokens that efficiently covers all text — common words intact, rare words decomposed.

Why not whole words?

English has millions of words. You can't give each one a number. Subword tokenization handles any word — even misspellings and neologisms — with a fixed vocabulary.

Why tokens matter

Every AI cost, speed, and context limit is measured in tokens. When Claude says "200K context window," that's 200,000 tokens — roughly 150,000 words.

A token is like a Scrabble tile, but some tiles have whole words on them and some have just fragments. The AI's vocabulary is its tile set — fixed at training time.

2. Embeddings — Meaning as Geometry

An embedding is a list of numbers (a vector) that represents the meaning of a piece of text. Words, sentences, or entire documents get mapped to points in a high-dimensional space where similar meanings are nearby.

"yoga" ● ● "meditation" ● "mindfulness" "gardening" ● ● "permaculture" ● "database" ● "SQL" ← Each dot is a 768-dimensional vector. Close dots = related meaning. This diagram shows 2 of 768 dimensions. →
How it works: An embedding model (like nomic-embed-text that you're running) takes text as input and outputs a fixed-length vector. Nomic produces 768 numbers per input. These numbers encode semantic relationships learned from billions of text examples during training.

Why 768 dimensions?

Human intuition works in 3D. Embeddings work in hundreds or thousands of dimensions because meaning is complex. "Yoga" is similar to "meditation" along one dimension (mindfulness practice), similar to "athletics" along another (physical activity), and similar to "India" along yet another (cultural origin). Each dimension captures a different facet of meaning.

Measuring Similarity

To find similar items, you compute the cosine similarity between two vectors — essentially measuring the angle between them. A score of 1.0 means identical direction (same meaning), 0 means unrelated, -1 means opposite.

cosine_similarity("yoga", "meditation")     → 0.82  (very similar)
cosine_similarity("yoga", "database")        → 0.11  (unrelated)
cosine_similarity("yoga", "physical fitness") → 0.71  (related)
This is what powers your semantic search. When you search "wren" in Gathering, the query gets embedded into a 768-dim vector. LanceDB finds the stored vectors closest to it. That's why "Ravi in the Snow" shows up — its embedding is geometrically near "wren" in meaning-space, even though the word never appears.

3. Neural Networks — Learning Machines

A neural network is layers of simple math operations that, stacked together, can learn complex patterns. Each layer transforms its input and passes the result to the next layer.

Output Layer
Final prediction — next token probabilities
Hidden Layer N
High-level patterns — concepts, relationships
Hidden Layer ...
Mid-level patterns — phrases, grammar
Hidden Layer 1
Low-level patterns — character combos, word parts
Input Layer
Token embeddings come in here

Weights and Parameters

Each connection between layers has a weight — a number that determines how much influence one node has on the next. When people say a model has "175 billion parameters" (like GPT-3) or "~2 trillion parameters" (estimated for GPT-4-class models), they mean the model has that many weights. These numbers are the model's knowledge — the learned patterns from training.

Activation Functions

Between layers, an activation function introduces non-linearity — without it, stacking layers would just be fancy multiplication. The most common today is ReLU: if the number is negative, make it zero; if positive, keep it. Simple, but it lets networks learn curves instead of just straight lines.

Weights are like the strengths of synapses in your brain. Training adjusts these strengths until the network produces useful outputs. The architecture is fixed; the weights are learned.

4. Transformers — The Breakthrough Architecture

The Transformer (2017, Google's "Attention Is All You Need" paper) is the architecture behind GPT, Claude, LLaMA, and essentially every modern language model. It replaced recurrent networks with a mechanism that processes all tokens in parallel.

The key insight: Previous architectures (RNNs, LSTMs) read text sequentially — word by word, carrying a summary forward. This made them slow and forgetful over long sequences. Transformers read all tokens at once and use attention to figure out which tokens matter to each other.

Positional Encoding

Since transformers process all tokens in parallel (not sequentially), they need another way to know word order. Positional encodings are added to each token's embedding to tell the model "this token is at position 5" vs "position 50." Without this, "the cat sat on the mat" and "mat the on sat cat the" would look identical.

Scale

Small Models

7B parameters (Mistral, LLaMA 7B). Runs on your Mac. Good for specific tasks, embeddings, local inference. This is what Ollama runs on your Bedroom Mac.

Large Models

70B-2T+ parameters (Claude, GPT-4). Requires GPU clusters. Broad knowledge, complex reasoning. This is what you talk to via API.

5. Attention — The Key Innovation

Self-attention is how a transformer decides which parts of the input matter to each other. For every token, it computes a relevance score against every other token, then uses those scores to create a weighted mix of information.

Example: "The cat sat on the mat because it was tired"

What does "it" refer to? A human knows it's "cat." Self-attention learns this by giving "it" a high attention score toward "cat" and low scores toward "mat," "on," etc.

Query, Key, Value (QKV)

Attention uses three projections of each token:

  • Query (Q) — "What am I looking for?" (the asking token)
  • Key (K) — "What do I contain?" (each candidate token)
  • Value (V) — "What information do I carry?" (the content to retrieve)

The attention score between two tokens is the dot product of Q and K. High dot product = high relevance. The output is a weighted sum of all Values, where the weights are the attention scores.

Attention is like searching a library. Your Query is the question, each book's title is a Key, and each book's content is a Value. You match your question against all titles, then read most carefully the books that match best.

Multi-Head Attention

One attention "head" captures one kind of relationship (e.g., grammatical subject). Models use multiple heads in parallel (32-128) so they can simultaneously track grammar, co-reference, topic, sentiment, and other patterns. Each head learns to attend to different things.

6. Training — How Models Learn

Training is the process of adjusting billions of weights so the model produces useful outputs. It happens in two major phases.

Phase 1: Pre-Training

The model reads enormous amounts of text (books, websites, code — trillions of tokens) and learns to predict the next token. This is self-supervised learning — no human labeling needed. The text itself provides the training signal.

The training loop: 
1. Show the model: "The capital of France is ___"
2. Model predicts: "London" (wrong — early in training)
3. Compute loss: how wrong was it?
4. Backpropagation: adjust weights to make "Paris" more likely
5. Repeat billions of times across trillions of tokens

Loss Function

The loss function measures how wrong the model's predictions are. For language models, it's cross-entropy loss — essentially, how surprised was the model by the correct answer? Training minimizes this surprise across the entire dataset.

Backpropagation

Backpropagation is the algorithm that figures out which weights to adjust and by how much. It works backward from the output error, computing the gradient (direction of steepest descent) for each weight. Then gradient descent nudges each weight in the direction that reduces the error.

Phase 2: Post-Training (RLHF / Constitutional AI)

After pre-training, the model can predict text but isn't helpful or safe. Post-training aligns it with human preferences:

RLHF

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. Humans rank model outputs. A reward model learns these preferences. The language model is then optimized to produce outputs the reward model scores highly.

Constitutional AI

Anthropic's approach for Claude. The model critiques and revises its own outputs against a set of principles, reducing the need for human labeling at scale.

7. Inference — How Models Think

Inference is what happens when you send a prompt to Claude or any model. The model processes your input through all its layers and produces a probability distribution over its vocabulary for the next token.

One token at a time: The model doesn't generate a whole response at once. It predicts one token, appends it to the input, then predicts the next token. This is called autoregressive generation. A 500-word response means ~700 forward passes through the entire network.

Temperature

Temperature controls randomness. It's a number that scales the probability distribution before sampling:

Low temperature (0.0-0.3)

Sharp distribution. The model almost always picks the highest-probability token. Deterministic, focused, repetitive. Good for code, facts.

High temperature (0.7-1.0+)

Flat distribution. Lower-probability tokens get a real chance. Creative, varied, sometimes incoherent. Good for brainstorming, fiction.

Top-k and Top-p Sampling

Top-k: Only consider the k most likely next tokens (e.g., top 40). Top-p (nucleus sampling): Only consider tokens whose cumulative probability reaches p (e.g., 0.95). Both prevent the model from picking extremely unlikely tokens while preserving some randomness.

KV Cache

Since each new token requires attending to all previous tokens, the model caches the Key and Value computations from prior tokens. This KV cache is why long conversations use more memory — it grows linearly with sequence length.

8. Context Windows — Working Memory

The context window is the maximum number of tokens a model can see at once — both your input and its output combined. It's the model's working memory.

Claude (Anthropic)

200K tokens (~150K words). Can process entire codebases or long documents in one shot.

GPT-4

128K tokens. Comparable range for long-context tasks.

Local models (7B)

Typically 4K-32K tokens. Smaller models usually have shorter context windows.

Your Chorus index

45K messages won't fit in any context window. That's exactly why you need embeddings + RAG.

The fundamental limit: Attention is O(n²) — every token attends to every other token. Doubling the context window quadruples the computation. This is why context windows have practical limits even as hardware improves.
Context window is like a desk. You can spread out 200K tokens worth of papers. Anything that doesn't fit on the desk, you can't see — you need to go get it from the filing cabinet (that's RAG).

Chunking

Documents need to be split into chunks before embedding — typically 256-1024 tokens each. Too small and you lose context; too large and the embedding becomes a vague average of too many ideas. Chunk boundaries matter: splitting mid-sentence degrades retrieval quality.

RAG vs Fine-Tuning

RAG keeps the model unchanged and injects knowledge at query time. Fine-tuning changes the model's weights. RAG is simpler, cheaper, and the knowledge is always up-to-date (just re-embed). Fine-tuning is for changing behavior, not adding knowledge.

10. Vector Databases — Searchable Meaning

A vector database stores embeddings and enables fast similarity search. When you have 45K embedded messages, you need a way to find the closest vectors to a query without comparing all 45K every time.

LanceDB (yours)

Embedded database — no server, just a library. Stores vectors as columnar data on disk. Fast for small-to-medium datasets. Zero infrastructure cost.

Pinecone / Weaviate / Qdrant

Server-based vector DBs for large scale. Managed services. Overkill for your current size — LanceDB is the right choice.

Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN)

Exact similarity search (comparing against every vector) is O(n). ANN algorithms (like HNSW — Hierarchical Navigable Small World) build graph indexes that find approximately nearest neighbors in O(log n). The trade-off: 95-99% recall for 100x speed.

Your search latency: Semantic search at 42-85ms across your Gathering corpus. That speed comes from LanceDB's columnar format and ANN indexing. Brute-force search over the same vectors would take 500ms+.

11. Fine-Tuning vs Prompting — Customization Spectrum

There's a spectrum of ways to customize model behavior, from lightweight to heavy:

Full Fine-Tuning
Retrain all weights on your data. Expensive. Changes the model deeply.
LoRA / QLoRA
Train a small adapter (~1-5% of weights). Cheap. Composable. Most practical fine-tuning today.
RAG
No training. Inject knowledge at query time. Always current. This is what you're building.
Few-Shot Prompting
Give examples in the prompt. No training needed. Limited by context window.
Zero-Shot Prompting
Just describe what you want. Simplest. Works for general tasks.

When to Fine-Tune

Fine-tune when you need the model to behave differently — adopt a specific style, follow a format, or handle a domain where prompting isn't enough. Don't fine-tune to add knowledge — use RAG for that.

Quantization

Large models are stored in 32-bit or 16-bit floating point. Quantization compresses weights to 8-bit or 4-bit integers, reducing model size 4-8x with small quality loss. This is how 7B models run on your Mac — Ollama uses Q4_K_M quantization by default.
