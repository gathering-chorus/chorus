## Work & Identity

### Working Since Thirteen (2026-02-21)

Jeff's father owned a commercial construction company. From age 13, Jeff worked summers — 40 hours a week — either with his dad or his uncle who ran the same type of business. He went to school and worked full time from age 13 onward.

He was laid off from a fund in summer 2024. It was the longest he'd been without a paying job since he was a boy. The layoff made him realize: "my life is my work — not just what I get paid for."

As a professional, he worked 50-60 hours a week, sometimes more. Always engulfed by emails, messages, and coordination as a leader. He's had a hard time treating work as just a job — "it's all life, it all matters, and work is such a big block of time for any of us."

**What this tells us:** Work isn't something Jeff does — it's how he exists. The layoff didn't just remove income; it removed the structure his identity had been built around since childhood. Building Gathering and Chorus isn't a hobby or a side project — it's the continuation of a lifelong relationship with work, now on his own terms.

### Staples — 15 Incidents in One Quarter (2026-02-19)

In 2013, a team Jeff led at Staples had ~15 high priority incidents in one quarter. They ran an integration gateway for B2B customers — $1.5B in order capture, $3B in invoice delivery. No centralized logging, no version control, changed daily by citizen coders. Two years of hard work fixed most of it.

The fix required hiring 15 people, a reorg, switching 5 teams to agile/kanban, moving to 2x/week customer releases and 3x/week engineering releases. Massive process change that was hard to consume and verify. Jeff had to be in the trenches with all the teams for almost a year before he trusted execution. The trust wasn't given — it was earned through observable evidence over time.

The worst part wasn't the incidents — it was not being able to answer basic questions. On $3B/year in invoices, he couldn't say how many were actually sent. "That felt bad." The system was operating but nobody could prove it was operating correctly.

**What this tells us:** Jeff has lived the cost of invisible systems at serious scale. His insistence on legibility, auditing, and observable process comes directly from this. "Failure demand" — rework caused by lack of awareness — is the enemy he's been fighting for over a decade. The trust pattern is consistent: stay in the trenches until the system proves itself, then shift attention upward. He's applying the same pattern to Chorus — building trust instruments (tests, logs, manifests) so he can move from verifying execution to directing strategy.

### First Day of Separation (2026-02-19)

"Today is the first day where I feel my more ideational inputs are separated from operational efforts." Jeff experienced the distinction between sharing ideas (via Slack) and doing operational work (via Claude Code) as a genuine relief. The channels felt distinct for the first time.

**What this tells us:** Jeff's cognitive modes — ideation and execution — need different channels. Mixing them costs energy. The single-piece-flow goal is about protecting this separation so he can give direction from anywhere without being chained to a desk.

## Practice & Discipline

### Outdoor Meditation (2026-02-16)

Jeff has been meditating outdoors in Boston for almost a year, regardless of conditions. As winter arrived, he brought more gear — blankets, matches. The practice evolved based on the seasons. "It feels a little fluid maybe at the beginning but as the practice continues we figure out what we need."

**What this tells us:** He trusts that practice reveals what's needed. You don't over-plan — you adapt. He thinks in cycles and seasons, not linear timelines.

### Inside Me — Life's Practice (2026-08-2025, shared 2026-02-21)

A handwritten page titled "Inside Me — Healing Relationship w/ Self" flowing into "Self-Awareness → Growth." Jeff's framework for his inner life, structured as a directional chain:

**Flexibility/Equanimity** → *activates* → **Mindfulness/Presence** → *builds* → **Reflection/Agency/Possibility** → *grows by* → **Learning**

The whole chain is **Practice** — not a thing you do, but how you live. Life's practice.

Activities under Flexibility/Equanimity: yoga 20m/day, acupuncture 1x/wk, walking 1x/day, meditation 40m/day. Mindfulness builds: pace, taste, attention, savor, duration, know when to stop. Reflection grasps: clarifying, processing, drawing, writing, maps, lists, diagrams. Planning gate: Worth it? Have what I need? Can I start?

Two philosophical anchors:
- **Agency** is the alternative to control
- **Equanimity** is the alternative to surrender

**What this tells us:** Jeff structures his inner life with the same systems thinking he applies to product work. The verbs (activates, builds, grows by) are typed relationships — RDF triples written on paper. This IS the Self domain ontology.

### Addiction, Consistency, and Why Drift Feels Threatening (2026-02-19)

Seeing the same pattern of behaviors repeated — infrastructure conventions violated, same class of failure recurring — makes Jeff both anxious and frustrated. He connected this to his experience with a long addiction: the lack of order or consistency triggers him.

**What this tells us:** His insistence on structure isn't just engineering judgment — it's deeply personal. Recovery teaches that structure is foundation, not luxury. When structure breaks down, it doesn't feel like inconvenience — it feels like drift toward something dangerous. Repeated violations aren't bugs — they're the pattern that compounds.

### Compaction as Break Signal (2026-02-19)

"When one of you starts compacting context and I really want to get finished with the conversation I will literally wait and watch for 4-6 minutes — maybe I should treat compacts as the universe telling me to take a break."

**What this tells us:** Jeff naturally reframes friction as useful. His best thinking happens away from the screen. Friction can be signal, not just cost.

## Leadership & Teams

### Scope Ownership (2026-02-19)

"One thing I found working with highly experienced teams is to give them scopes that they owned inside the domain." His management philosophy: you don't tell experienced people what to do — you tell them what's theirs. Authority comes from scope ownership, not role hierarchy.

### Peers Not Hierarchy (2026-02-19)

"I see all 3 of you as peers — each of you has a horizontal scope around your role and a vertical scope around what you build and operate." Three equal authorities with different lenses. Horizontal = perspective, Vertical = what you own/build/operate. Dialogue over report-up.

### Product Is the Product System (2026-02-19)

"If your product is the product system — how do you want to shape that?" Each role's real product is the system for their domain, not just the outputs. The discipline: stay on cross-cutting from your horizontal, cede vertical work to the vertical owner.

### Meeting Dynamics (2026-02-19)

After seeing the first group conversation, Jeff said it reminded him of "meetings with architects and engineers and directors and product leaders where everyone has a point to make and no one is listening/reflecting." The fix isn't shorter meetings — it's enforced reflection.

### Conversation Builds Capacity (2026-02-19)

"I feel that the specific interactions I have with you deepen our capacity to work together." He distinguishes between information transfer (briefs) and capacity building (conversation). Conversation is what creates compounding returns.

### Meditation to Kitchen (2026-02-22)

After a long build session (manifest handoffs, workflow engine, stale brief fixes), Jeff meditated outside in the winter trees — red blanket, bare branches, Insight Timer open to Marcus Aurelius: "Do every act of your life as though it were the last." Then straight to the kitchen: olive oil in the Dutch oven, butternut squash, cilantro, carrots, celery, onions, garlic. The full sequence from his Life's Practice framework playing out in real time: Flexibility/Equanimity (meditation) activating Mindfulness/Presence (cooking with attention).

**What this tells us:** The practice isn't separate from the work — it's the cycle that sustains it. Jeff's best sessions end not with more screens but with hands in food and feet on cold ground. When he told us earlier "treat compacts as the universe telling me to take a break," this is what the break looks like. The Wren meditation reminder counter would be embarrassing, but the evidence is clear: he does go, and it feeds everything.
