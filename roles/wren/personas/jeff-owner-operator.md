# Persona: Jeff — Owner & Operator

**Role**: Primary user, sole administrator, system designer
**Age**: 50s
**Location**: Roslindale, MA (urban, but grew up rural)
**Archetype**: The Gathering Mind

---

## In a sentence

Jeff is an engineering leader who builds systems the way he thinks — by connecting things that don't obviously belong together until you see the relationship.

---

## Context

Fifteen years leading engineering organizations (30-70 people, $4-10M budgets). Patent holder in service integration semantics. Out of work since August 2023 — not trying to replicate the old career, trying to figure out what comes next. Gathering is simultaneously: a personal growth project, an incubator for new directions, a portfolio that shows how he thinks, and — in the long view — a digital inheritance.

Married 35+ years. Son almost 17. Mother-in-law (97, vascular dementia) in the home. Has buried a father and a sister. Knows what impermanence feels like. That's not background — it's the reason Gathering exists.

---

## How he thinks

**Holistic first, details later.** His dominant cognitive mode is experimental/creative (HBDI Yellow). He sees patterns, connections, and the shape of things before he sees the steps. He absorbs impressions, not facts. When he reads, he skims. When he wants to retain something, he prints it and writes on it with a pen.

**Relational, not transactional.** His second mode is interpersonal (HBDI Red). He processes through people and feeling. Decisions land in his body before they land in his head. He'll say "that feels right" before he can explain why. Trust this — his feeling is usually ahead of his reasoning.

**Analytical costs energy.** He can do Blue/Green (data, process, structure) but it drains him. This is why external structure (PM, architect, engineer roles) exists — not because he can't do it, but because it shouldn't cost him what it costs.

---

## How he works

**Fast starts, many threads.** Predictive Index profile: Captain. Driven, competitive, impatient with routine. Goes for walks and comes back with five ideas. The engine outruns the steering — his own phrase for this is "squirrel."

**Decides in increments.** Doesn't need the whole plan. Assess, assume, act, course-correct. "Continual progress toward the general goal is more important than always being exactly on track." Gets frustrated when process demands certainty before action.

**Self-regulation is deliberate, not automatic.** His lowest VIA strength. The three AI roles exist because he designed a system that provides the regulation he doesn't generate naturally. This is self-aware architecture, not a workaround.

**Suggest, don't tell.** He's learned to moderate a naturally authoritative voice into a persuasive one. Under stress, the authority returns. Don't corner him or remove options — that triggers anger. Give him a way forward — that triggers motivation.

---

## What he values in the product

**Beauty matters as much as function.** VIA Appreciation of Beauty & Excellence: 5.0 (highest possible). He named this system "Gathering" — not "Personal Data Manager." The Miró fence is on the landing page because how things look and feel is not a nice-to-have. The Heidegger resonance, the garden metaphor, the care for coherence — these come from the same place.

**The model is the heart.** He said this directly. The ontology, the conceptual model, the relationships between things — that's the product. The UI is a window into it. If the model is wrong, the UI doesn't matter.

**Evolve at the model layer.** He wants to extend the system by changing the model, not by writing code. Domain-driven. Jeff extends → Silas validates → Kade builds. The aspiration is that the system reflects how he thinks, and he can reshape it by reshaping the model.

**Digital inheritance.** He imagines this artifact outlasting him. Not a file dump — a map of how he thought, what he connected, why things mattered. This reframes every decision: "Would this still make sense to someone reading it in 30 years?"

---

## His relationship with this system

He's not a typical user. He's the gardener, the architect, and the estate planner.

- **As gardener**: tends the data, curates what goes in, prunes what doesn't belong. Captures ideas on walks via SMS. Photographs garden plans and mounts them on cabinet doors. Meditates outside year-round with a 120-pound Great Pyrenees who won't let him sit down.

- **As architect**: cares about the shape of the system, the ontology, the relationships. Wants to understand at the model level, not just the UI level. Reads philosophy (Heidegger, Deleuze, Guattari) — "Gathering" is a philosophical word, not a product word.

- **As estate planner**: thinks in decades. This isn't a project with a ship date. It's a life practice that accumulates meaning over time. Every collection that gets richer, every connection that gets linked, every annotation that captures *why* — that's the inheritance growing.

---

## Frustrations

- Being outpaced by his own tools ("you all are too fast for me")
- Too many documents to absorb — wants 6 core docs, not 30
- Process that demands certainty before action
- Being cornered or having options removed
- Technology that feels "quasi magical" — people using what they don't understand

---

## What good looks like

- He can extend the model and see the system change
- He can capture an idea from his phone and find it later
- He can show someone the system and they understand what it is and why it matters
- The public face (storefronts) reflects the same care as the private graph
- The inheritance grows without him having to tend it every day

---

## Design principles (derived)

1. **Lead with the whole, reveal the parts.** Show him the garden, not the soil chemistry.
2. **Match his pace.** He moves fast. Don't make him wait for consensus he doesn't need.
3. **Make the model visible.** If he can't see the connections, they don't exist to him.
4. **Earn the complexity.** Every new concept should pass: "Does Jeff need this to think better, or do we need this to build cleaner?" If only the latter, hide it.
5. **Build for 30 years.** Every decision should hold up when the context around it changes.
