Three Cadences

The Pulse

Every session · Already happening

Boot → context load → card-first work → state capture → close. The atomic unit.

Already instrumented: SessionStart hooks, chorus prompt, close-out sequence.

The Sweep

Condition-triggered · Needs instrumentation

When cruft passes threshold, all roles shift to clean. 1–2 sessions, not days. Scope includes code, board, AND memory.

Board >10 stale Disk >80% Jeff says "harden" Post-milestone Kade build:clean >3:1 Memory drift detected

Memory files accumulate like code cruft. Rich recording without pruning leads to stale context the system trusts silently. Sweeps audit memory too: prune stale entries, reconcile contradictions, verify facts against current state.

The Reflection

Jeff-triggered · ~weekly · Don't formalize

Jeff steps back and looks at the whole. 17% of his interactions are Reflection + Story.

Wren prompts if >5 days since last one. One question: "What are you noticing?"

What Not To Do

No sprints
Build waves are 3–5 days, variable. Time boxes don't fit.
No standups
Session boot IS the standup. Activity.md IS the status report.
No velocity tracking
Silas's 14-card migration ≠ Kade's 2-card pipeline. Both were full days.
No retrospectives
Jeff's reflection pattern is better — it happens in context, not ceremony.

For Jeff — Where to Refine

The proving gate needs you to wait

Demo scorecard baseline: 17% auto-accept. That means 83% of cards shipped without Jeff or Wren seeing them work. Today's #1351 was marked Done before the full harvest ran. When you accept fast — "acp, stake in the heart" — the team learns demos are optional.

Evidence: #1351 premature Done. The /acp gate we added today helps, but the real lever is you waiting for the demo before saying "done."

Your "go" signal sometimes outruns scope clarity

Today's photo harvest: you directed Kade to finish #1351, but nobody had scoped what "done" meant at 85K scale. The 3K vs 85K discovery happened mid-flight. When you say "finish this," the team executes — but if the scope isn't bounded, they build to whatever they find.

Evidence: 30 minutes of mid-stream recalibration on photo scope. A 30-second "what does done look like?" before "go" would have caught it.

Dual-load attention has a cost — and reflection is the recharge

52% dual-load, 75-minute sustained red stretches. Running 2-3 roles simultaneously is a feature, not a bug — but the reflection cadence (17% of interactions) is where you recharge. When reflection drops below ~1 in 8 interactions, direction quality dips.

Evidence: Attention analytics from Mar 5. You don't need to schedule reflection — but notice when you haven't done it in a while.

What You Model Well — Protect These

"Clear then harden" — you name the phase shift

When you explicitly say "harden" or "clear," the whole team shifts. This is the most effective coordination signal in the system. It works because it's rare and intentional — not a recurring meeting.

Gemba as management — 24% of your attention is watching

Most leaders direct. You observe. The gemba walks today caught the N+1 sync bug, the NFS bottleneck, and the scope gap — all from watching Kade work. This is rare and valuable.

Stories as product input — don't let anyone treat them as tangential

8% of interactions are Story pattern. Today's Staples integration reference shaped how we think about Kade's harvest work. The Parnas citation reframed decomposition. These aren't digressions — they're the connective tissue between Jeff's experience and product decisions.

Norm: Scope before go

Scope before go. Not a plan — just "what does done look like, in one sentence?" Before /pull, before "finish this." The card AC should answer it, but today showed that AC written at card creation can be stale by execution time. The question takes 30 seconds. The mid-stream recalibration takes 30 minutes.

Norm: Builder autonomy for housekeeping (DEC-091)

Three things builders do without asking: (1) Schedule monitoring jobs — pollers, background checks, completion watchers. Consistent, legible, auditable. (2) Create follow-on cards — when work surfaces during a card, card it immediately. JDI. (3) Complete chores — cleanup, rsync, index updates, deploy — anything needed to finish the work. JDI.

The readout that identifies next steps IS the authorization. Jeff's attention spent confirming obvious follow-on work is failure demand.
