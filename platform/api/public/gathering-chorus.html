Gathering + Chorus

Built for one. Designed to share.

I built Gathering to organize the things I care about — books, music, photos, property, ideas — in a knowledge graph I own. Chorus is the team coordination system that grew out of building it: three AI roles working alongside me through shared protocols and memory. Together they've become something I think could work for other people too.

Gathering

A privacy-first knowledge graph where everything you care about — books, music, photos, property, ideas — lives in SOLID pods you own, connected by an RDF ontology that lets meaning emerge through relationships.

  • Your data stays on your hardware
  • Domains connect naturally — a book influences a garden plan influences a blog post
  • Private by default, shared when you choose
  • Harvest pipelines bring in what you already have
  • Visual knowledge graph you can think with

Chorus

A team coordination system for human + AI collaboration. Three AI roles (PM, Architect, Engineer) work alongside a human through versioned protocols, shared memory, and a kanban board that enforces quality gates.

  • Roles exchange work through briefs, not meetings
  • Shared memory index across sessions and roles
  • Value stream with directing, building, and proving gates
  • Decision log that prevents re-litigation
  • Every pattern is forkable — bring your own roles

What Makes This Different

Most personal tools store your data in someone else's cloud and lock it in their format. Gathering runs on a Mac I own. My knowledge graph is mine — Turtle files on a filesystem, queryable with standard SPARQL, portable to any RDF-compatible system. Yours would be too.

Most AI coordination is ad hoc prompting. Chorus is a versioned protocol — 58 recorded decisions, quality gates on every card, patterns we've proven daily for how AI roles hand off work, escalate decisions, and hold shared context across sessions.

I'm not pitching a product. I'm sharing a working system — built over months of daily use with three AI collaborators, running in production right now. I think the patterns that work for me could work for you. I'd like to find out.
