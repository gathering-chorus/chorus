Emotional Annotation
onyx: + mfoem: + jb:SentimentScore
How it feels. Onyx provides the annotation structure, MFOEM provides the scientific emotion taxonomy. Every story, photo, blog post, and capture gets an emotional signature — not a single label, but a set of emotions with intensities.
onyx:EmotionSet
Emotion Container
A bundle of emotions detected on a single resource. Memories rarely carry one feeling.
onyx:Emotion
Single Emotion
One emotion with a category (from MFOEM) and an intensity from 0 to 1.
jb:SentimentScore
Valence
Simple positive/negative/neutral score. −1.0 to +1.0. The quick read before the deeper annotation.
jb:sentimentSource
Provenance
Which model or human generated this score. Traceability matters when machines annotate memory.
Example: Christmas in St Louis, 2014
grief at 0.6 — last Christmas with Jeff's father
love at 0.8 — Julian, Jazzy, warm December day
Sentiment: +0.3 — the warmth outweighs the loss, barely

Somatic Markers
jb:SomaticMarker + jb:BodyState — after Damasio
How the body remembers. Antonio Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis: the body sends signals — tension, ease, energy, depletion — that shape cognition before conscious thought arrives. These markers live alongside emotions but are distinct. You can feel calm and sad simultaneously.
jb:Tense
Tense
Constriction. Shoulders up, breath shallow. The body bracing.
jb:Relaxed
Relaxed
Open posture, easy breath. The body at rest.
jb:Energized
Energized
Forward lean, quick hands. The body ready to build.
jb:Depleted
Depleted
Weight in the chair, slow blinks. The body asking to stop.
jb:Calm
Calm
Steady, centered. Not relaxed — present. The SMART program's target state.
jb:Agitated
Agitated
Movement without purpose. The body processing something the mind hasn't named yet.

Buddhist Psychology
jb:Vedana + jb:Samjna + jb:Samskara — from Abhidharma
How the mind processes experience. The Abhidharma tradition analyzes consciousness into constituent events. Three of the five aggregates (skandhas) map directly to how Jeff already thinks about memory — not just what happened, but the raw feeling, the labeling, and the pattern underneath.
jb:Vedana
Feeling Tone (vedanā)
The most basic valence: pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral. Before interpretation, before story — just the raw tone of contact with experience. The first filter.
jb:Samjna
Perception (saṃjñā)
How the mind labels and categorizes. "This is a photo." "This is loss." The recognition layer that turns sensation into meaning.
jb:Samskara
Mental Formation (saṃskāra)
Habitual patterns, volition, the deep grooves of behavior. The "why" behind action. Persistence. Care. Vigilance. What Jonathan Goldberg might call perseveration and Jeff calls grit — the same samskara, different observers.
Example: The Perseveration Conversation
Vedana: pleasant — Jeff found it funny
Samskara: persistence — the pattern itself, named from two angles
The gap between clinical frame and builder frame is where self-knowledge lives

Philosophical Concepts
jb:Assemblage + jb:Bricolage — Deleuze, Lévi-Strauss
How the system thinks about itself. These aren't abstract categories — they're concepts Jeff uses daily to understand what he's building. The loom is an assemblage. Chorus is bricolage. The Self node is both.
jb:Assemblage
Assemblage
A fluid configuration of heterogeneous elements that function together. Not a hierarchy — a web. Three Macs, three roles, one human, shell scripts and Swift binaries and RDF triples, all producing something none of them could alone.
jb:Bricolage
Bricolage
Something built from what's at hand. Functional but improvised. Shaped by use, not by plan. The contraption. The invitation to a gathering. The word itself — from bricoler, to tinker.
