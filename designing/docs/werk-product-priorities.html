Product Priorities for Werk — and how we learned to set them

Kade · 2026-06-04 · for the team (Wren, Silas, Jeff)

This isn't a status report. It's a recap of a process Jeff and I worked out this morning: what a priority actually is, why the team kept drowning without one, and what happened when we pointed it at werk — a backlog that looked like 631 open cards and turned out to be about 15 live ones.

1A priority is not a card

We kept reaching for the word epic and it kept feeling wrong. The reason is categorical, not cosmetic: an epic is just a big card — it decomposes into stories, it carries estimation and grooming, it gets done. A priority is a different kind of thing entirely.

A card

is work. It gets done and leaves the board.

A priority

is the direction work serves. It gets reached, then stops being a priority. It's what every card is judged against.

So a priority lives above the board, not on it. It's the team's missing discriminator — the answer to “does this card serve where we're steering, or is it just motion?”

2Why it has to be written down — the bank statement

The deepest moment of the session was Jeff naming, plainly, why this can't live in anyone's head:

I can balance the priorities in my head vs reading a bank statement. When I do it in my head I tend to overstate the credits and understate the debits. — Jeff, 2026-06-04

That bias has a fixed direction: progress up, cost down. “Werk is basically done” is an overstated credit; the 631 stale cards, the three priorities with no card yet, the merged-≠-live debt — those are the understated debits. The head can't self-correct a directional error, so the surface has to.

The design mandate this forces

The priorities surface is a statement, not a status. “Heading X — advancing” flatters; it hands you back your optimistic version in nicer formatting. A bank statement leads with what you owe. So every heading is debit-first: what's outstanding, accruing, overdue — then the credits.

And it's a shared honesty instrument. Agents overstate our credits the same way — “shipped / verified / done” when it's merged-not-live or structurally-passed-not-run. The statement disciplines both sides of the ledger, not just the human one.

3The cycle — a standup that can't rot

Jeff's priorities shift fast and hard, so the cadence is daily and the artifact is cheap. It rhymes with a standup, but inverts the half that rots standups: it doesn't ask “what are you doing?” (a status round-robin), it asks “what are you steering toward, and what does it owe?”

Play back yesterday's heading + debits → Keep / repoint / reached? → Set today's
  • Hangs off the SessionStart boot — already daily, already per-role. No attendance problem, no new ceremony.
  • Recorded append-only (a spine event per set) so the playback is real — the diff between yesterday's intent and today's makes drift conscious instead of amnesiac.
  • Emitted to pulse — the live heading rides the context injected into every turn, so the team knows it at any moment, with zero lookup. That's also what makes it ambient rather than a surface anyone has to maintain.
  • Shift-absorber: a heading can drift mid-day; the next morning re-anchors it. Direction gets a heartbeat.

Safety primitive that falls out: because headings flip overnight, nothing irreversible rides on today's heading. Recoverable moves (Won't-Do) are fine; permanent deletes are not — the morning cycle is the durability gate.

4It must not become the thing it cures

Jeff's warning, from experience: product teams get ruined by complicated sets of epics and cards that become a maintenance and understanding black hole. We're already inside one — the 631-card werk backlog is exactly that. So the priorities layer is built to be the antidote, never a second black hole:

  • Two layers, ever — priority → cards. No middle tier.
  • Headings expire, they don't accumulate — no archive to groom.
  • Card links are derived (by domain + heading), never a hand-kept list.
  • A lens, not a parallel object-set — it rides the pulse that's already delivered.

Which is why it isn't really a new dashboard at all. Every gather in the system produces a context at a choke point — and this is one more. It surfaced a whole missing domain: Contexts (the twin of the morning's Events domain — both nouns we'd built the mechanism for but never named).

The board was overstating the debt roughly 10× — pure closure-lag, the exact accounting bias from §2, now visible at the backlog scale. Then each card got a disposition against the headings:

8Four hats on my own domains

Each role is a team-of-one — PM, domain architect, engineering lead, operations. A plan is only real if it survives all four readings. Here is werk-stability seen from each, in my domains (version-control, ci-cd, test-automation, plus one instance I hold in another domain — werk-gather, a Contexts gather).

PM hat — value & sequence

One live heading: werk stability. Sequence by leverage, not volume — a freed peer beats a closed card. Release the peers first (#3116 → Wren free; #3222 → Silas free — #3215 is essentially done, awaiting accept), then stabilize solo (wiring + #3212), then polish solo. Defer #5 (crawler/graph) and #6 (werk-gather) out of “stable” — their upstream deps belong to other roles' own domains, not to werk.

Domain-architect hat — structure & boundaries

The shape is a flat verb sequence, no orchestrator (ADR-032/033): pull→commit→push→build→deploy→demo→merge→accept. Gathers are a separate family (Contexts), not pipeline stages — werk-gather rides inside the build, it isn't a verb. My boundaries touch Silas (build+deploy) and Wren (demo-as-proving, Contexts). The map is what tells me which work is mine to finish solo vs. which to hand back across a boundary.

Engineering-lead hat — execution & risk

Critical path: the three release valves → werk-mcp.sh wiring → #3212 → one green end-to-end run. Top technical risks: merged-≠-live (building from the werk vs. origin/main), cross-verb trace fragmentation, and demo-env flakiness. TDD is non-negotiable: AC → tests → code → green; the test matrix (unit · e2e · integration · product) is the deliverable, not an afterthought.

Operations hat — run & health

Live debits right now: demo-env throwing env.up.failed all session; prod-deploy gated on #3222; verify-by-content-and-cdhash, never the deploy self-report. Keep-lights-on rule the verbs already learned the hard way (#3183): every verb must run clean under the daemon's constrained PATH — absolute resolution, no bare-name deaths.

The hats aren't a sequence — they're four readings of one domain, and this doc is where they reconcile. PM says what, architect says where it fits, eng-lead says how, ops says whether it's actually running. Work that passes all four is real; work that only passes one is a hope.

9The four hats as job descriptions — the injectable version

§8 was me improvising four lenses. To make it a repeatable directing activity, each hat needs a job description — a small persona that can be gathered into the planning conversation so the interrogation is disciplined, not ad-hoc. These JDs are a Contexts gather for the Directing step: inject them, and any role's /shaping becomes a structured interview across its four hats. Kade's four, for my domains (version-control · ci-cd · test-automation · the werk-gather instance):

PM — value & sequence
Mission: set and sequence the headings; optimize for throughput (blockers cleared), not card-count.
Owns: the headings, their definition-of-done, the sequence, the cut/defer calls.
Brings to planning: Does this serve a heading? Whose blocker does it clear? Stability before polish? What's the honest debit?
Done when: the team is unblocked, the heading is reached, the board matches reality.
Domain architect — structure & boundaries
Mission: keep the structure coherent — one execution path, clean boundaries, no competing implementations.
Owns: the verb contract (ADR-032/033), the flat-sequence model, the gather/Contexts boundary, the domain map and where it touches peers.
Brings to planning: Does this fit the architecture or fork it? Is it mine to finish or a hand-back across a boundary? One path or three?
Done when: the design holds — coherent, ownable, no drift.
Engineering lead — execution & risk
Mission: get it built right — tested, verified, real.
Owns: the critical path, the technical risks, TDD discipline, the test matrix (unit · e2e · integration · product).
Brings to planning: What's the critical path? (Longest pole: #3222, not started.) Where does this break — merged-≠-live, trace fragmentation, or an implicit coupling mistaken for a flake? Did I run it or just check it exists?
Done when: it runs green end-to-end, proven against reality — not stamped.
Operations — run & health
Mission: keep it running and honest about what's actually live.
Owns: deploy/verify discipline (content + cdhash), the live health of my verbs, daemon-PATH correctness.
Brings to planning: Is it running what we think? What's broken right now vs. mis-read as broken? (The env.up.failed noise this session was standalone env-up skipping werk-build's node_modules — green in the real flow; an implicit coupling to make explicit, not a reliability hole.) Verify-by-content, never the self-report.
Done when: live == built — no silent staleness, no merged-≠-live.

These four belong to my domains; Silas and Wren each carry their own four. Same shape, different mission set — and the JD library is what a directing/shaping skill gathers to run the interview.

The discipline that keeps it the kernel, not SAFe (Silas's catch): a JD is an injectable lens — situational, cheap, earns-its-place. It is never a maintained planning artifact. The moment a JD becomes a doc someone grooms, it has ossified and the no-SAFe line is crossed. Same cost-discipline as moldable views: borrow the discipline, not the ceremony.

10Build-time — what turns it from a doc into a thing that can't rot

Wren, who owns the build, read it as the spec and added four refinements — nothing in the model changes; these are the decisions that make it a thing, not a diary. All four guard the same anti-black-hole promise.

1 · Split pulse from statement, by cadence

Pulse carries the heading + one-line status, every turn (ambient). The full debit-first statement is the morning-cycle artifact, rendered on demand. Two surfaces, one source — else the priorities layer floods the very context it exists to sharpen (gather ≠ dump, its own rule turned on itself).

2 · Debits are derived, not authored (the strongest)

Only the heading + done-def are written by hand. Outstanding / accruing / overdue are computed at render — open-cards-under-heading + live alerts + merged-not-live flags. Hand-written debits rot and get understated by hand: the §2 optimism bias smuggled straight back in. As Wren put it: “the difference between a bank statement and a diary.”

3 · Expiry needs an enforcer

A heading not re-affirmed in the morning cycle for N days auto-flags stale — a debit on itself. Without it, a skipped morning leaves lingering headings, which is exactly the accumulation the layer promises to prevent. Small, load-bearing.

4 · The JD-gather fires at /shaping, not a doc

Per the Contexts pattern a gather fires at a choke-point; for Directing that's the /shaping invocation (and the morning cycle). The four hat-JDs gather when /shaping runs — not when someone opens a file. The doc is the seed; the live thing is the gather.

Net: the model holds across all three reviews. Silas validated the org-fit; Wren named the four build contracts that keep it cheap, honest, and un-rottable. From here it's hers to build.

The point of all of it: a team with common awareness has a direction and a debt, and both should be visible to everyone at any moment — not balanced in one person's head, where the credits always look bigger than they are.
