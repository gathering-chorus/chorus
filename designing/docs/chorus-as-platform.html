Chorus — A System to Build Systems

What it is — for someone meeting it for the first time: a potential adopter, a collaborator, or anyone trying to compose a team of humans and AI agents that holds together without a person in the middle of every message.

Chorus is a platform, in the oldest sense of the word: its product is not a feature, it is a method of building. It is a system that builds systems — and the people building inside it happen to be AI agents, working alongside one human. Everything it produces is downstream of a single bet: get the way you build right, and what you build follows.

1 · The product is the method

Most software is a set of features. Chorus is the machine that forges software — the repeatable cycle that takes an idea and carries it all the way to a working, observed, reflected-upon change. What it would hand to someone else isn't its applications; it's the forge.

The proof that this is more than a slogan: the system generates large parts of itself. The same model that describes Chorus is the model it uses to generate its own interfaces, its own tests, and the scaffolding of its own actions — from one source. (The logic inside those actions is still written by hand; what's generated is the shape, the contract, the wiring.) A system that can regenerate its own structure can build anything shaped like it.

2 · A small grammar: nouns, verbs, events

Underneath everything is a grammar small enough to fit in a sentence: the system is made of nouns, verbs, and events.

  • A noun is a thing — a domain, a product, a piece of work. It has structure and identity. (Some nouns are objects; some are processes turned into things, so they can be measured.)
  • A verb is an action — commit, deploy, register, run. A small, fixed set, kept small on purpose.
  • An event is what a verb gives off when it fires: the signal that something happened. Events are how the system sees itself.

The decisive move is that nouns and verbs are peers — neither owns the other. This isn't the usual arrangement where a thing has its methods. It's two independent lists, and you compose them: a noun uses a verb when it needs one. "Commit" belongs to no single kind of work; anything that needs to commit, uses it. You build a verb for the first thing that needs it, and you generalize it the moment a second thing needs it — never before.

And you compose on need, not in advance. You don't pre-build every combination of noun and verb; you wire one to the other where there is an actual demand. That single rule — compose on need — is what keeps the system from sprawling into the thing it's trying to replace.

If that sounds like ecology, it should. A noun is an element; a verb is a function it performs. The two oldest principles of permaculture design turn out to be the two directions of this same grid: each element performs many functions (a noun does many verbs), and each function is supported by many elements (a verb serves many nouns). Redundancy by design — and that is where the resilience comes from.

3 · How it builds — a cycle, not a pipeline

The method is a cycle, run over and over: shape an idea → direct it → design it → build it → prove it → reflect — and shape the next thing. Each step moves a piece of work from one state to the next by running it through a sequence of verbs. A "flow" is just a noun routed through verbs — the same shape as a factory line or an integration pipeline.

The cycle isn't rebuilt per project. It is generated from the model and applies to any stream of work — building a change, forging a new piece of the model, running the team's daily rhythm, reflecting on the system's own health, bringing outside data in. Same cycle, different content.

4 · Build the connections; rent the conductor

Running a flow has two layers, and Chorus treats them very differently:

  • Integration — the wiring that lets a verb act on a noun and pass the result to the next step. This Chorus builds: one uniform interface that every action plugs into.
  • Orchestration — the conductor that decides which verbs run, in what order, with what checks. This Chorus rents. Because every verb is uniform on the same interface, a conductor can drive them without bespoke glue — today an off-the-shelf build runner for code and a scheduler for time-based rhythms; a data-flow engine for ingestion is the same pattern, not yet wired.

The discipline: don't build an orchestration engine. Build the connective tissue once, keep it uniform, and choose the right conductor per problem. The model makes that choice legible — every stream of work records how it's triggered (by an event, a clock, a continuous flow, incoming data), which names the tool to reach for. (Today that's a human reading the field, not yet an automatic selection.)

5 · One standard, many owners

The data isn't designed by one architect. Each domain's owner defines that domain's own data — the person closest to it knows it best. What's held in common is the standard every definition must meet, not the definitions themselves. It's a hard-won lesson from large-scale enterprise integration: a single architect designing everyone's data becomes a bottleneck that loses the very expertise it was meant to gather. The anti-pattern, avoided on purpose: one owner designing all the data, everywhere.

6 · How the team is composed and organized

This is the part most worth taking, because it's the transferable one: how do you compose and organize a team of AI agents — and one human — so it coheres without the human becoming the bottleneck?

Composed. A few AI agents, each a distinct and persistent role with its own character, plus one human as the owner. A role is defined not by a job title but by what it is accountable for — the nouns it owns (its domains) and the verbs it performs (its actions). It's the same peer grid from the grammar, now at the scale of people: roles own elements, and the functions over them.

And each role is two things at once: it runs its own streams of work, and it helps build the shared platform every stream uses — security, observability, the model, the tooling. Stream-aligned and platform, in one small team.

Organized. Work moves through the cycle, and the roles coordinate by emitting and consuming events — one role's output is another's input — rather than a human carrying messages between them. Each role offers its capabilities to the others as a service they can call, not a queue they wait on. Each owns its own domain's data, to one shared standard. The effect is a team whose coordination lives in the system, not in a person's head — which is the only way a team of agents stays coherent when the human steps away.

7 · The influences, made usable

Four unrelated fields independently describe this structure — which is the strongest sign it's a real pattern, not one team's invention. But they earn their place here not as theory; as patterns you can take and apply, especially to how a team is composed. Each, with its example and its takeaway:

Permaculture
each element, many functions
A role isn't single-purpose. One role builds, tests, and ships — the way a single chicken gives eggs, pest control, and fertile soil. Take: don't carve roles into single tasks; let each serve several functions, and let each function be covered by more than one role. That redundancy is what carries the team when a role is busy, or wrong.
Team Topologies
platform as a service
Shared capabilities — observability, security, the model — are offered as self-serve services the other roles call, not a ticket queue they wait in. Take: organize a team for flow — make internal capabilities consumable on demand, so no one is blocked waiting on another's attention.
Cybernetics
close the loop
The team runs on a shared event stream: one role's output becomes another's input. A feedback loop, not a chain of hand-offs through a manager. Take: wire outputs to inputs directly and let the system route the work — sense, respond, repeat — instead of a person dispatching every step.
Rhizome
no center
Any role connects to any other directly; none is the router — and, the real point, neither is the human. Take: decentralize coordination. The fastest way to slow a team is to make one member — or the owner — the bus every message must pass through.

The engineering practice running underneath all four is Extreme Programming — small releases, test-first, integrating continuously, pairing. The lenses say why this shape; XP is the disciplined how to build inside it.

Four languages, one law — but the usable version is plainer than that: compose roles that each do many things; offer what you build as a service; close the loop so the system routes the work; and keep the human off the critical path of coordination.

8 · The honest frontier

The hard, unsolved part — worth naming plainly — is self-regulation. All four lenses describe a system that holds its own health: a garden that tends itself, a regulator that corrects itself, a network that heals its own connections. Chorus has the structure of that system, but not yet the reflex. Today, when something drifts, a human still tends to notice first. Closing that gap — making the system sense and correct its own operational health before a person has to — is the frontier the entire design is pointed at. Saying so is what keeps the rest from being a brochure.

9 · What you'd adopt

If you took Chorus, you wouldn't be taking an app. You'd be taking the method — the system-to-build-systems — and configuring it to your own streams of work. The deliverable isn't software; it is a repeatable, instrumented, legible way to forge software, with a small team — human and agent — working the cycle.
