Gathering — A Personal Knowledge Graph with Agency

Last updated: 2026-03-06 by Wren (PM) Status: Living document — reflects current system state

What It Is

Gathering is Jeff Bridwell's externalized mind — a garden of meaning built on semantic web principles.

It's a memory layer that sits across his entire digital life — books, photos, music, blog posts, properties, ideas, projects — and makes the connections between them visible, searchable, and meaningful. Like a garden, the value isn't in any single plant. It's in the ecosystem: how things grow together, what feeds what, what blooms in which season.

Content stays where it lives. Gathering owns the memory of what Jeff has, when it mattered, and how things connect. A book that influenced a garden plan. A photo from a trip that connects to a blog post. A song linked to a memory. Seeds from different sources, planted in the same soil, growing roots between them.

Not a website. Not an app. A living model of your world that you can think with. A private incubator where ideas, memories, and projects gestate until they're ready to be released into the world.

Core Principle

Privacy-first, publish-when-ready. Everything starts private. The workshop is not the storefront. Things graduate from private to shared to public when they're ready — a blog post pushed to social media, a static site launched for a business idea, a collection shared with a friend.

This is a political decision, not just a technical one. When surveillance is the default business model and people voluntarily install recording systems in their homes, owning your data locally is a counter-architecture — like the Dutch buying Manhattan from the indigenous people for beads, except now the beads are smart speakers and doorbell cameras, and what's being purchased is attention, behavior, and identity. Colonization no longer requires physical presence.

Data stays local. Intelligence is rented. Concentric trust: Self = local AI only, Team = Chorus (cloud-rented intelligence, locally-stored context), App = hybrid. The architecture encodes values, not just engineering constraints.

Who It Serves

Jeff. One person. This is not a platform, not a product for others, not a social network. It's a personal tool built by and for someone who thinks in connections, carries a lot, and needs a system that thinks with him — not just stores for him.

Gathering also serves a broader purpose. Jeff has been out of work since August 2023 and is deliberately not returning to the same career shape (leading large engineering orgs). Gathering is four things at once:

  1. Growth investment — professional and personal development through building with current technologies
  2. Career incubator — exploring what a different shape of work looks like, with Gathering as potential seed
  3. Capability portfolio — demonstrating not just technical skill but systems thinking, philosophical grounding, and the ability to see across domains
  4. Digital inheritance — a record of how Jeff thought, what he connected, and why things mattered. Not a file dump — a map of a life lived deliberately. The Feeling layer is what makes this an inheritance, not an archive.

This means the public-facing dimension matters. Not everything graduates — but what does should demonstrate how Jeff thinks, not just what he builds.

The Philosophical Frame

Heidegger — Gathering as Versammlung

From Heidegger: meaning coheres through relations, not central control. A thing is defined by what it gathers, not its material. The name "Gathering" comes from Heidegger's concept of Versammlung — how meaning, world, and being come together without being imposed by a subject (DEC-010).

The RDF/ontology foundation is exactly right for this because relationships are first-class. You're not storing rows in tables — you're building a graph where anything can connect to anything else, and those connections can emerge over time as you discover them. The pods are your extended mind on disk. Fuseki lets you ask questions across it.

Buddhist Dependent Origination

From Buddhist philosophy: gather without reifying — the system stays workable because nothing in it is fixed. Nothing has independent existence; everything arises in relation to everything else. This maps directly to RDF triples — subject, predicate, object. No node is meaningful alone. The graph IS the meaning.

Foucault — The Fascism in All of Us

From Foucault's preface to Deleuze & Guattari's Anti-Oedipus: "The fascism in us all...that causes us to love power, to desire the very thing that dominates and exploits us." The surveillance economy operates on this principle — we voluntarily adopt the systems that extract our data, attention, and agency. We buy the beads. We install the cameras.

Gathering is a counter-architecture. Not a protest — a practice. Building the alternative rather than critiquing the default. Data sovereignty as daily discipline, not political statement.

Gestell — The Enframing

From Heidegger's concept of Gestell (enframing): modern technology reveals everything as standing-reserve — resources to be optimized, extracted, consumed. People become "human resources." Attention becomes "engagement metrics." Identity becomes "user profiles."

Gathering refuses this frame. The knowledge graph treats Jeff's experiences, memories, and connections as meaning — not data to be mined. The privacy architecture isn't a feature; it's a philosophical position about what technology is for.

Digital Detritus — The Technosphere's Blast Radius

Every service you've ever touched deposits artifacts into your life — documents, photos, contacts, messages, metadata. It accretes silently across years: 250K Google Docs you didn't know existed, photos in four places, contacts duplicated across platforms. This is digital detritus — the accumulated residue of the technosphere crossing your personal boundary without your awareness or consent.

The boundary between a person and the technosphere is like an iceberg. What you see — the apps on your phone, the files on your desktop — is the visible fraction. Below the waterline: cloud storage you forgot, exports you never reviewed, services that kept your data after you stopped using them. You navigate around something you've never mapped.

Gathering is sonar. The harvest pipeline maps the submerged surface — the first honest inventory of your digital blast radius. Not to organize it, not to optimize it. To make the boundary observable, and then shapeable. To let you decide what's yours, what serves you, and what's just residue.

This connects directly to concentric trust: Self/Gathering/Chorus aren't just data tiers — they're boundary decisions about where you end and the technosphere begins. The graduation model isn't just a privacy feature — it's the mechanism for consciously managing that boundary. The wall stays up until you open the gate.

Sartre — Expression as Knowledge

From Sartre's Being and Nothingness: pre-reflective consciousness isn't truly known until it's made an object of reflection. You can live something without knowing it. Carrying isn't knowing.

The self-portrait (below) IS that move — taking pre-reflective experience (the red zones, the tension, the hedging) and making it an object Jeff can see. Gathering extends that act into a system. The knowledge graph is the expression that makes the knowing real. Without it, Jeff carries all of this — but the graph externalizes it, makes the connections visible, lets him think with it instead of just through it. The pod files are reflective consciousness made durable.

This connects to Foucault's concept of hupomnēmata — the ancient practice of self-writing as a technology of the self. Not journaling for therapy, but writing as a practice of constituting yourself as a subject. Gathering is a 21st-century hupomnēmata — except the writing is structured as RDF triples, and the self being constituted is a knowledge graph.

The Garden Frame

Gathering is a garden, not a factory. The metaphor isn't decorative — Jeff is a passionate gardener, the ontology already models gardens, and the lifecycle of a garden maps precisely to how the system works:

  • Seeds — raw ideas, notebook scribbles, captured photos, L0 references. They enter the soil unformed. Not everything germinates.
  • Planting — committing a seed to the graph. Giving it structure, a URI, a place in the ontology. The act of saying "this matters enough to tend."
  • Tending — curation. Adding connections, annotations, personal meaning. The daily work of the knowledge graph. This is where the AI thinking partner helps — surfacing what needs attention.
  • Growth — ideas developing into projects, connections multiplying, metadata enriching over time. Growth is not linear — it's seasonal, cyclical, sometimes dormant.
  • Harvest — already the named pattern. Gathering metadata from external sources into the graph. But also: the moment when something grown in the garden is ready to be used, shared, or offered.
  • Fruiting — readiness for graduation. The content has matured. It's ready to move from private to shared or public. The gardener decides when to pick — the system never forces ripeness.
  • Compost — ideas that don't graduate aren't failures. They decompose and feed the soil. A parked idea informs a future project. A dismissed AI suggestion still enriched the context. Nothing is wasted.
  • Seasons — each pass through data → security → features → automation is a growing season. The garden looks different each cycle, richer each year.
  • Perennials — long-running themes that return season after season (books, property, the blog). The root system is deep.
  • Annuals — one-off projects, experiments, things that bloom once and feed the compost.

The garden is the private space. The market is the storefront. Content grows in the garden before it moves to the market — if it moves at all.

Seeds and Glimmers

Within the garden, new inputs follow a taxonomy:

  • Seeds have grown into something — the Paulo Dorow conversation became DEC-073 (co-located embeddings), the Athena project at Staples became the completeness model for Chorus
  • Glimmers are signal that hasn't landed yet — observations, connections, ideas that might germinate or might compost

This distinction matters because it honors both the things that grew and the things that didn't — without treating the latter as waste.

The Gap Between Them

The gap between the two images is the product space. The axis is reactivity → reflectivity:

  • The portrait (as-is) is reactive: experience drives behavior before reflection can intervene
  • The fence (to-be) is reflective: disparate things gathered with care, intentionally composed, beautiful because it was deliberate
  • Gathering is the system that adds the middleware between experience and reaction — creating space to see, connect, and choose before acting

The spectrum:

  • Over-encapsulation: Everything stays private forever. The garden never opens. The portfolio purpose fails. Psychologically: suppression, rigidity, inaccessibility.
  • Under-encapsulation: No privacy model. Raw data leaks to public. Psychologically: reactivity, emotional flooding, boundary violations.
  • Healthy encapsulation: Capture everything (feel fully). Surface connections (observe clearly). Graduate when ready (respond intentionally). The graduation model.

Five Feature Domains

Derived from the self-portrait's output layer: Integration, Doing, Feeling, Connecting, Collecting.

1. Collecting — The Harvest

Gather metadata from external sources into the graph. Each harvest brings new seeds into the garden — some will be tended into rich connections, most will remain cataloged and searchable.

Sources: Apple Photos (1M+), Apple Music (100K+ tracks, 5K+ albums), Facebook, LinkedIn, WordPress (41 posts), local media (178TB), books (3-5K physical). Target: 15-25M triples.

2. Connecting — The Root System

Cross-domain relationships via RDF ontology. The graph sees what no single service can. Like roots beneath the soil, connections are invisible from the surface but they're what holds the garden together. Measured by cross-domain connection ratio — if this drops as sources are added, the garden is accumulating topsoil without deepening roots.

The "Self" domain doesn't exist as a separate collection — it exists only as the connections between domains. Stories link to people link to practices link to values. Without Self, the other domains are catalogs. With Self, they're a knowledge graph about a person.

3. Integrating — The Soil

The semantic memory layer. SOLID pods as source of truth, Fuseki as query engine, ontology as shared vocabulary. The soil holds everything — nutrients, structure, memory. Privacy-first graduation model: Private → Shared → Public. The garden wall protects what's growing.

4. Feeling — The Tending

Personal annotations, ratings, "when it mattered." Not just metadata — memory. The gardener's knowledge of what each plant needs, which ones are thriving, which are struggling. This is what makes it an extended mind instead of a catalog — the care invested over time.

Jeff's "Songs I Love" blog (jeffbridwell.blog, 41 posts, 2019-2024) is the Feeling domain before he knew he was building one. The arc: uncertainty → personal stories grounded in music → loss and recovery → principles and manifestos. Water imagery throughout. The people he's lost form the spine. Addiction told honestly but through music. This is what Feeling looks like when it's working — raw experience made meaningful through curation and connection.

5. Doing — The Lifecycle

Seeds → seedlings → growth → fruiting → harvest → compost. In system terms: capture → idea → project → release → archive. Some things are perennials. Some are annuals. Some seeds never germinate — they feed the compost. The lifecycle is cyclical, not linear. Nothing is wasted.

Plus: The Thinking Partner — The Gardener's Companion

Conversational AI that sees the garden whole, notices what's ready, what's struggling, what could connect. Not a chatbot. Not a search engine. The answer to "no teacher" in the self-portrait — a companion that helps the gardener see the garden he's already growing. Architecturally necessary for tending at scale (15-25M triples).

The Graduation Model

The garden wall. Everything starts private. Graduation is always the gardener's choice.

  1. Private (inside the wall) — Only Jeff. The growing space. Seeds, experiments, half-formed ideas, personal memories. Default for everything.
  2. Shared (the gate) — Invited guests see specific beds. Read or read+write grants to specific people/groups per collection. Showing a trusted friend what's growing.
  3. Public (the market) — Open web. No login required. What Jeff chooses to offer the world — the fruit, not the process.

Examples of graduation:

  • Light Life Urban Gardens: Garden data lives in the property collection (private working version); the static site is the public face (graduated artifact)
  • Blog posts: Drafted and refined privately in WordPress, published to social media when ready
  • Akasha Studio: First outside-in consulting deliverable — built from the inside, offered to the world

The system never pushes toward the market. The wall stays up. The gardener opens the gate.

Second Product: Chorus

Gathering is the app. Chorus is the team coordination product — coordination-as-code. Where Wardley mapped conversational programming as the next evolution after serverless (one human talking to one AI to produce code), Chorus is the step beyond: one human directing multiple AI agents through a shared repository of meaning. The artifacts — decisions, briefs, spine events, board state, interaction patterns — encode how a team thinks together. The repository IS the product.

Three qualities: versioned (like code), legible (like prose), auditable (like infrastructure). The distinction from conversational programming (DEC-088): we're not making programming conversational — we're making team coordination programmable.

Three surfaces: Loom (the team — people, ownership, activity), Werk (the protocol — spine events, card flow, gates), Chorus (the memory — search, sessions, decisions).

Interaction Architecture

Nine interaction patterns govern how Jeff and the team work together: Direction, Ideation, Demo, Triage, SWAT, Gemba, Clearing, Story, Reflection. These form a three-layer architecture:

  1. Conversation layer (Control Plane) — Wren detects the active pattern, injects context, emits spine events
  2. Card layer (Work Plane) — /pull, /demo, /acp gates activate based on the pattern
  3. Spine layer (Data Plane) — every gate emits events, Borg surfaces what the data says

Concurrent patterns are normal — Jeff routinely operates in Story with Wren, Demo with Silas, and Building with Kade simultaneously. Three roles, three modes, one person. This is the desk freedom goal realized.

Honest Assessment

The ambition is coherent, not scattered. Everything connects back to one idea: externalize what Jeff carries, make the connections visible, graduate what's ready. That's not scope creep — it's a unified vision with a lot of surface area. One garden, many beds.

The foundation is real. Ontology, pods, Fuseki, authentication, observability, team coordination — they work. More "Functional" and "Solid" than "Missing" on the capability map. The soil is prepared. The perennials are established.

The risk isn't technical. It's sequencing. With one person and finite energy, the danger is planting everything at once and having nothing to show at harvest. The concentric circles pattern is the right instinct — each growing season focuses on one ring. Tend what's planted before breaking new ground.

What's working now: The interactions feel cleaner. Deploys have almost no friction. The team coordinates through briefs and board state, not through Jeff relaying messages. Jeff operates in multiple conversation patterns simultaneously — chatting with one role, demoing with another, building with a third. The desk freedom goal is approaching reality.
