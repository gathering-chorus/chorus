WHY — the worldview5 sources · a living system

Permaculture — the regenerative ethic

Bill Mollison & David Holmgren (1970s–2002) · 3 ethics + 12 design principles · gateway: Toby Hemenway, Gaia's Garden

3 ethics: earth care · people care · fair share (return of surplus). 12 principles include: observe & interact · catch & store energy · obtain a yield · apply self-regulation & accept feedback · use & value renewable resources · produce no waste · design from patterns to details · integrate rather than segregate · use small & slow solutions · use & value diversity · use edges / value the marginal · creatively respond to change.

It is a design grammar for systems that sustain themselves on their own throughput — outputs re-enter as inputs, the whole is tended rather than operated. This is the literal top of the Chorus principle stack — and not as a metaphor: in the live principles graph, each of the ~32 Chorus-specific principles carries a parents edge up to one of Hemenway's permaculture principles (the ~14 roots marked isPermacultureParent). The parentage is modeled, queryable structure, not rhetoric — though the roots are Hemenway's principle set, a superset of Holmgren's twelve listed above.

maps to → a codebase as a regenerative ecosystem: closed loops (no waste), incremental always-earning change (small & slow / obtain a yield), composability (integrate not segregate), and closed control loops that correct without a human at the lever (self-regulation).

permacultureprinciples.com · Holmgren, Principles & Pathways Beyond Sustainability (2002) · open.oregonstate.education/permaculture

Heidegger — the stance toward technology

The Question Concerning Technology (Die Frage nach der Technik, 1953/54), Lovitt trans.

The danger is Gestell (en-framing): modern technology's mode of revealing that "challenges forth" nature and sets everything upon as Bestand (standing-reserve) — optimizable resource on call. The dammed Rhine becomes hydraulic pressure; "human resources" become Bestand too. The alternative is older: technē as poiēsis — a "bringing-forth," a revealing (alētheia), kin to art, that lets a thing be what it is.

"But where danger is, grows the saving power also." (Hölderlin)

His later counter-figure to Gestell is Versammlung — gathering. A genuine thing gathers a world around it (the fourfold) rather than flattening it into resource; dwelling is letting that be. Gelassenheit = releasement, the free relation to technology.

maps to → build so a primitive gathers a meaningful locale (a card, a session, a role gathers its context and its people) instead of reducing everything to one more optimized metric. This is literally why the team chose -gather over -inject: "inject" is the Gestell push; "gather" is Versammlung. The word must match the frame because the frame is the essence.

Lovitt trans. (UCI/Monoskop PDFs) · SEP: Martin Heidegger §5.2 · The New Atlantis, "Understanding Heidegger on Technology" · Wikipedia: Gestell

Deleuze & Guattari — the architecture

Anti-Oedipus (1972), A Thousand Plateaus (1980)

A machine (machine désirante) is "a system of interruptions or breaks (coupures)" in a flow — it cuts an upstream flow and is itself a flow downstream, so "every machine is a machine of a machine." Desire is production, not lack. Machinic ≠ mechanical: a mechanism is closed with a fixed function; a machine is open, defined by its connections and flows, existing only in the act of connecting.

An agencement (lossily Englished as "assemblage" — better: arrangement / fitting-together) is a multiplicity of heterogeneous parts cohering by their relations, defined by what it does, not what its parts are. The rhizome (vs. the tree): any point connects to any other, acentered, running on "and… and… and…" Movements of deterritorialization / reterritorialization undo and re-fix codings (the wasp and the orchid).

maps to → Chorus is an agencement machinique: roles + human + hooks + spine + git + MCP, cohering only through connection and flows — each a cut in a flow that is a flow for the next. Rhizomatic (any role connects to any other; self-heals along new lines), it runs on coupling, not a tree of authority. The nudge is a flow-coupling; a rename is a deterritorialization that must reterritorialize on one word, one path. Coherence comes from collapsing competing paths into one connective flow — not from adding hierarchy.

SEP: Gilles Deleuze · Wikipedia: Rhizome / A Thousand Plateaus · Nail (2017) on agencement vs. assemblage · Anti-Oedipus pp.36–41

Zen — the practice

Chan/Zen Buddhism · (wu-wei is Taoist, deliberately excluded)

shoshin (初心, beginner's mind, Suzuki): "in the beginner's mind there are many possibilities; in the expert's, few." mu (無, Jōshū's dog, Gateless Gate Case 1): not "no" but a one-word barrier that refuses a wrongly-framed question. mujō (無常, impermanence) + non-attachment: clinging is "self-binding without a rope" — gripping what has already changed. Direct pointing (直指人心): truth grasped by immediate experience, no abstraction between you and the thing. Everyday mind / "chop wood, carry water"; kanso (簡素, simplicity = strip clutter to reveal essence, max effect / min means).

maps to → decide from what the system is now (live state, the running binary's real cdhash) — clinging to "it's deployed / it's green" is the mujō error, the state already moved. Non-attachment = let the dead thing go: prune code, deadhead stale cards, delete the workaround once the fix lands. Directness + kanso = one path, fewer layers, no padding, no ceremony. mu = refuse a wrongly-posed card rather than over-engineer an answer to it.

SEP: Japanese Zen Buddhist Philosophy · Suzuki, Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind · Gateless Gate Case 1 · Layman Pang (chop wood/carry water)

Homeostasis — the mechanism

Bernard (milieu intérieur, 1865) → Cannon (coined "homeostasis," 1926/1932) → cybernetics (Wiener 1948; Ashby's homeostat / ultrastability)

A system holding internal variables near a setpoint despite perturbation, via negative-feedback loops (sense deviation → controller acts to counter it). "Homeo-" = steady, not static — a regulated equilibrium. Wiener generalized negative feedback as the common control principle "in the animal and the machine." Ashby's ultrastability is the higher tier: when essential variables leave their viable band, the system reconfigures its own control parameters until stability returns — adaptation without a pre-programmed solution.

maps to → the precise model for "no human is the thermostat" — which is the target, not today's state: right now Jeff is still often the thermostat. What's real today is the low tier (self-healing restarts, kickstart-on-crash, scheduled idempotent fixes); ultrastability — the system re-deriving its own thresholds when a metric leaves its band — is where we're going, not what holds. The test is identical in biology and software: is equilibrium held by the loop itself, or by a person at the dial? Naming the gap honestly is how it closes.

Billman, "Homeostasis…" (Front. Physiol. 2020) · Gross, "Bernard to Cannon" · Ashby, homeostat / ultrastability (Macy Conferences) · Wiener, Cybernetics (1948)

HOW — the craft6 disciplines · how you build one

Each operationalizes a facet of the worldview — and several are not borrowed but Jeff's own career (semantics especially).

Thinking in Systems — Donella Meadows

Stocks, flows, feedback loops (balancing = goal-seeking/stabilizing; reinforcing = self-amplifying); delays cause overshoot. Her 12 leverage points run from weakest (12: constants/parameters) to strongest (1: transcend paradigms) — the counterintuitive thesis being that the obvious knobs are weakest, while rules → goals → paradigm are strongest.

operationalizes → homeostasis & D&G: the practical theory of feedback. Patching parameters is the lowest leverage (locally-rational, globally-pessimizing — the suboptimization disease); intervene at rules/goals to regenerate the system's own self-correction.

Meadows, "Leverage Points" (1997) · donellameadows.org · Thinking in Systems (2008)

Lean — Toyota Production System & Lean Software (Poppendieck)

Muda (7 wastes) · flow · pull · jidoka (stop-the-line: halt the instant a defect appears, build quality in at the source) · kaizen. The Poppendiecks' 7 software principles: eliminate waste · amplify learning · decide late · deliver fast · empower the team · build integrity in · see the whole (optimize the whole, not the part). The "plank roads" story — cheap-to-lay, rot-fast infra as the metaphor for technical/infrastructure debt — is theirs.

operationalizes → permaculture: produce-no-waste + self-regulation. jidoka is a homeostatic stop; pull prevents over-production; "see the whole" is the anti-suboptimization stance. Plank roads is permaculture's warning in engineering dress.

Poppendieck, Lean Software Development (2003) · Wikipedia: Lean software development · leanessays.com

Agile — the Manifesto (2001)

Four values (left over right): individuals & interactions · working software · customer collaboration · responding to change. Twelve principles include early/continuous delivery, welcoming late change, frequent working software, sustainable pace, technical excellence, "simplicity — the art of maximizing work not done" (YAGNI), self-organizing teams, and the regular retrospective.

operationalizes → Zen + small-and-slow: short cycles + retrospection form the balancing loop that keeps the team adapting; presence and the now over the grand plan.

agilemanifesto.org · agilemanifesto.org/principles.html

XP — Extreme Programming (Kent Beck)

Values: communication, simplicity, feedback, courage, respect. Practices: TDD (red-green-refactor) · pair programming · continuous integration · small frequent releases · refactoring · simple design / YAGNI · collective ownership.

operationalizes → the practice/discipline. Refactoring is pruning — continuous removal of decay; TDD + CI are the tight feedback loop catching defects at the source (the same reflex as jidoka); pairing is literally how this team works; TDD is DEC-1674.

Beck, Extreme Programming Explained (1999/2004) · Wikipedia: Kent Beck

Semantics — RDF/OWL, ontology, the canonical model

Jeff's lineage: the Staples canonical-model discipline · Cambridge Semantics (Sean & Simon Martin) · Athena

RDF (subject-predicate-object triples) · OWL (classes, properties, constraints, inference) · SPARQL · the knowledge graph. "Meaning as structure": an ontology isn't documentation about the data — it is the data's definition, formally enough that a machine reasons over it. The canonical data model collapses integration from n² point-to-point mappings to 2n (each system maps once, in and out) — the model as the contract. Cambridge Semantics (Sean Martin, CTO; brother Simon, VP Eng; ex-IBM) built Anzo on exactly this; acquired by Altair 2024.

operationalizes → Heidegger / "ontology is architecture": the model is the spec the system is generated from, code a projection of it — not the source of truth. (The OWL-is-the-spec slice, #3242.)

W3C (RDF/OWL/SPARQL) · enterpriseintegrationpatterns.com: Canonical Data Model · Wikipedia: Cambridge Semantics

Automation — self-running systems

Google SRE: eliminate toil (manual, repetitive, automatable work; the 50% rule). Idempotency is what makes continuous unattended drift-correction safe — "run the fix script every 15 minutes without fearing damage." The automation hierarchy tops out not at better automation but at self-healing: "systems that don't need any automation — the database notices problems and fails over without human intervention." Infrastructure-as-code = declarative desired-state.

operationalizes → homeostasis: idempotent fixes on a schedule + declarative spec + self-healing as the design for a system that senses drift from its setpoint and corrects. The intended end state is "the human sets the setpoint; the system holds it." Partly real today (scheduled idempotent fixes, self-healing restarts), partly still aspirational (closed-loop drift-correction across the board) — stated as direction, not a current guarantee.

How it grows — Gall's Law

"A complex system that works is invariably found to have evolved from a simple system that worked. A complex system designed from scratch never works and cannot be patched up to make it work — you have to start over with a working simple system."
— John Gall, Systemantics (1975)

Chorus doesn't get more complex by being architected bigger. It gets more complex the way a garden does — and that is the convergence point of half the influences above, all describing the same mechanism of how complexity arises:

  • Systems thinking (Gall / Meadows) — complex-from-simple-that-worked; never from-scratch.
  • Permaculture — "use small & slow solutions; grow by chunking — build on your successes."
  • Deleuze & Guattari — "every machine is a machine of a machine": assemblages compose upward from working couplings.
  • Evolution / biology — complexity by recombination and exaptation: a part that worked for one thing (logs for debugging) repurposed into a higher function (events for observability). Nothing designed whole; everything elaborated from what survived.

Which is why the right move keeps being recognize and unify what already works, not build new — that's not luck, it's the mechanism of systemic evolution. The event spine evolving out of the structured logs that already worked is Gall's Law, live.

The pair: chunk & prune

Two forces, both required — variation and selection:

CHUNK (+)
grow by adding small working systems
PRUNE (−)
cut competing / overgrown growth

Chunk without prune → accretion (the thicket: 750 literals, two pipelines fighting). Prune without chunk → stagnation. Together → complex and coherent. It's evolution's own shape: variation (chunk) + selection (prune).

Pruning is a growth act, not a loss

A gardener doesn't prune to shrink the plant — they prune to redirect energy into the strong wood, and it grows more, better-shaped, from a sound base. So "coherence is one execution path" is really a pruning instruction: cut the competing growth so complexity re-grows clean. A /prune verb — selection pressure applied on purpose, making Gall's Law a daily act instead of a crisis discovered when the thicket is already on fire — is the proposed move here (not yet a built skill; today pruning is manual: deadheading stale cards, deleting workarounds once the fix lands). (merged≠live was Gall's inverse: two complex systems, neither evolved from a working simple one, both fighting — the cure was never a grander design, it was pruning back to one path and letting it re-grow.)

Where the influences disagree — one real tension

A frame where every source nods along is a coherence engine flattering itself — post-hoc rationalization wearing eleven hats. So here is a place these influences genuinely pull against each other, not a place they agree:

D&G — integrate, don't segregate
add connections; any node to any node; rhizome
vs
Zen/Lean — prune; one path
cut competing connections; collapse to one

One influence says add edges (richer assemblage, more coupling, more diversity); the other says cut edges (kanso, no-competing-implementations, one execution path). Taken naively they contradict — maximize connection vs minimize it. A doc that listed both as "things Chorus believes" without noticing would be exactly the flattering mirror.

How Chorus resolves it — the two operate at different layers, and that's the actual design rule: integrate at the model layer, prune at the execution layer. The ontology is maximally connected (one canonical model, every concept relatable to every other — the rhizome, the n²→2n collapse by connecting through one shared model). The execution path is maximally pruned (one way to deploy, one way to nudge, no competing implementations). Rich semantics, lean execution. The tension is real; resolving it by layer is what keeps "integrate everything" from becoming spaghetti and "cut everything" from becoming disconnected silos. (This is the reader's takeaway-with-teeth: when you're tempted to add a second deploy path "to integrate," that's the model-layer instinct misapplied to the execution layer — wrong layer for connection. Prune there.)

The synthesis

Chorus is a living system (the worldview) built with practitioner craft (the craft) — and the craft is not citation, it is Jeff's own discipline (the canonical-model work from Staples, Cambridge Semantics, Athena), turned on the team itself. Rooted in a regenerative design philosophy at the principle level, the work — coherence, the model, de-bespoking — is not cleanup. It is permaculture practice: pull the rotted planks, lay no more, grow durable integrated infrastructure that sustains itself.

(The commercial framing — "the field is in a plank-road boom; Chorus is positioned to be the road when the planks rot" — is a real claim but a different audience and a different argument. It belongs in a positioning piece that earns it, not in this DNA doc. Pulled here on purpose.)
