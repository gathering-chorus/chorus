Observing Value

April 5, 2026 — Wren + Jeff, Sunday reflection session

The Question

Jeff proposed a new card tag: origin:reflective vs origin:reactive. Not "planned vs unplanned" — that's logistics. Reflective vs reactive captures posture: did we step back and choose this work, or did something break and we responded?

This led to pulling the full board history — 1,604 cards across all Vikunja projects — and classifying every one.

What We Found

All time: 65% reflective, 34% reactive. One third of all work ever done was responding to breakage.
Last 7 days: 49% reactive. Nearly half. The system is demanding stabilization.

What Jeff Said

"I didn't know about a lot of our deeper issues."

The reactive work was invisible because roles fixed things fast enough that Jeff never saw the board-level trend. He felt the friction — the re-nudges, the "why is this broken again" — but the system never showed him the receipt.

"We did a lot of hand waving and saying it works."

"It works" meant "it compiled" or "the test passed." Not "Jeff used it and it held up." The 51% reactive week is the receipt for that gap — every card titled "fix X" is a card that passed its demo and broke later.

"I just chose to do more stuff."

Instead of stopping and saying "this isn't working." The storm maker pattern — pushing through friction by adding more work on top. 440 cards in the week of March 9th. That's not a team building. That's a team running.

"I thought our operations and logging and code structure were much better than they were."

Because demos said "tests pass, AC complete." The code review Kade did found 14 issues. Four copies of the same function. A 1,776-line shim file. Shipped as "done" through the proving gate. Nobody looked until someone looked.

The Insight

This session produced a new measurement instrument: reflective vs reactive ratio by layer. It answers a question the board couldn't answer before: where is our work going, and is it chosen or forced?

The dependency stack says top depends on bottom. The data confirms it: when bottom layers break (Infrastructure, Protocol), reactive cards cascade upward into Apps and Loom. Building at the top while the bottom is unstable generates the 51% reactive weeks.

The framework sequence Silas is running is the right response — stabilize the middle of the stack so the layers above stop generating reactive work from structural drift.

Stabilization priority reads directly from the data: Apps first (59% reactive), then Infrastructure and Protocol (mid-30s%), then the rest holds. Shared Awareness and Framework are already stable — low reactive ratios, mostly chosen work.
