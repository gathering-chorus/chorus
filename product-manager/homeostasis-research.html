Timeline of Thinkers

Domain Deep Dives

▸ Body Biological & Physiological Homeostasis 7 thinkers
Claude Bernard
1813–1878
Introduction to the Study of Experimental Medicine (1865)
Named the milieu intérieur — the constancy of the internal environment is the condition of a free life.
Walter Cannon
1871–1945
The Wisdom of the Body (1932)
Coined “homeostasis” and mapped the canonical feedback architecture: sensor → comparator → effector → loop.
Peter Sterling
1940–
Allostasis (1988); What is Health? (2020)
Allostasis: stability through change. The body anticipates and shifts setpoints based on predicted demand. The setpoint is a moving target.
Bruce McEwen
1938–2020
Allostatic Load (1993)
Cumulative physiological wear from sustained regulation. Measurable, predictive of cardiovascular disease, cognitive decline, mortality.
Herbert Benson
1935–2022
The Relaxation Response (1975); SMART program
The physiological opposite of fight-or-flight. A distinct, inducible parasympathetic state — not “calming down” but a coordinated hypothalamic response.
Lisa Feldman Barrett
1963–
How Emotions Are Made (2017)
Body budgeting: the brain as resource manager, tracking metabolic costs, issuing allostatic adjustments before deficits occur. Affect is body budgeting made conscious.
Karl Friston
1959–
Free Energy Principle
Brains minimize prediction error by both updating models AND acting on the world. Homeostatic regulation is one instance of a universal principle.
▸ Systems Cybernetics, Complexity & Resilience 6 thinkers
Norbert Wiener
1894–1964
Cybernetics (1948)
Generalized feedback: any system with sensor + comparator + effector exhibits homeostasis. The principle transcends substrate.
W. Ross Ashby
1903–1972
An Introduction to Cybernetics (1956)
“Only variety can absorb variety.” A regulator must match the variety of disturbances it faces. Design for response variety, not known failures.
Donella Meadows
1941–2001
Thinking in Systems (2008)
Stocks & flows, leverage points, system traps. Delays in feedback loops cause overshoot. Observability 5 min stale = corrective action fights a past state.
C.S. Holling
1930–2019
Resilience and Stability (1973); Panarchy (2002)
Resilience is not maintaining a single stable point but maintaining identity across disturbance. The adaptive cycle: exploit → conserve → release → reorganize.
Nassim Taleb
1960–
Antifragile (2012)
Beyond resilience: systems that improve from disturbance. Requires slack — a loaded system can’t learn from stress.
Stuart Kauffman
1939–
Santa Fe Institute; NK Networks
Self-organization: systems at the right connectivity spontaneously exhibit stable attractors — homeostasis without a thermostat. Edge of chaos.
▸ Organizations Teams, Production & Coordination 6 thinkers
Stafford Beer
1926–2002
Brain of the Firm (1972)
Viable System Model: five recursive subsystems (S1–S5). Homeostasis at every scale simultaneously. S5 balances exploit (S3) vs explore (S4).
Taiichi Ohno
1912–1990
Toyota Production System (1978)
Solved stability-adaptability tension by making correction fast and local. Jidoka: machines detect and stop. Andon: sensing made visible. Pull systems.
Amy Edmondson
1959–
The Fearless Organization (2018)
Best teams report MORE errors because they have safety to surface them. Psychological safety is the precondition for Andon behavior.
John Seddon
1954–
Freedom from Command and Control (2003)
Failure demand: demand caused by failure to do something right the first time. Measure demand type before optimizing — efficiency on failure demand is waste.
Pierre-Paul Grassé
1895–1985
Stigmergy (1959)
Coordination through shared environment modification. The agent acts on the environment; the modified environment guides the next agent. Briefs are pheromone.
Simon Wardley
1967–
Wardley Maps (2020)
Components evolve genesis → custom → product → commodity. Organizations calibrate immune response to one speed — genesis killed by efficiency antibodies.

Andon
